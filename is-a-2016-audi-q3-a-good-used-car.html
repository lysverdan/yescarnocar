Vehicle Intelligence · 2016 Audi Q3 2.0T quattro

Is a 2016 Audi Q3 a Good Used Car to Buy?

Yes — with conditions. A cheap Audi is a psychologically dangerous purchase, but this one's risks are specific and knowable: the chain tensioner, the water pump, and the oil consumption.

GOOD CAR — WITH CONDITIONS

Why it's a good car

The first-generation Q3 was already a mature design when it reached North America — underneath, it is essentially a slightly tall Audi A3 on the older pre-MQB architecture shared with the Golf Mk5/Mk6 era and the first-generation VW Tiguan, so most of the platform's teething problems were long since sorted. Its 2.0T (the earlier transverse version of VW/Audi's EA888 engine, 200 hp, shared with the Mk6 GTI and first-gen Tiguan) drives through a conventional six-speed automatic — a regular torque-converter unit, not a dual-clutch — and a Haldex-type quattro system that is simple and well-suited to winters, asking little more than periodic fluid changes. A loaded example is a genuinely enormous amount of vehicle for used-Civic money.

Does it have a timing belt or a timing chain?

A timing chain — and the chain tensioner is the headline on this engine family. Early versions of this 2.0T had tensioner failures that could let the chain jump timing on an interference engine, which means bent valves. The revised tensioner design predates this car's production, so a 2016 should carry the updated part from the factory — but at higher mileage the chain itself can still stretch, so a cold-start listen and, ideally, a part-number check remain the most important tests on the car.

The things to check before you buy

  • Cold start Get there before the seller runs it. A few seconds of rattle or chain-slap from the front of the engine on a stone-cold start is the tensioner/chain talking — the single most important sound on the car.
  • Water pump and thermostat The plastic water pump housing weeps coolant with age, and the failure window is right where a higher-mileage car sits. Look for crusty residue and dampness around the pump area, and treat an original pump as a when-not-if replacement.
  • Oil consumption and PCV This engine's oil-consumption route is usually a failing PCV valve or breather hose. Ask the seller directly how often they top up between changes — around half a litre per 1,000 km is normal-ish for this family; a litre or more needs investigating before purchase.
  • Carbon buildup Direct injection means the intake valves coke up over time; a walnut-blast cleaning is due on a higher-mileage car that has never had one.
  • Transmission and Haldex fluid The six-speed automatic and the Haldex AWD unit both want periodic fluid services despite any lifetime-fill talk — ask for records.
  • Suspension and rust Front control-arm bushings are a known wear point by higher mileage, and on a salt-belt car the rear subframe mounts, trailing-arm pickups, and brake lines need a proper look.

What would make you walk

  • A sustained cold-start rattle A rattle that lingers or returns on warm restarts points to the chain system on an interference engine.
  • Heavy oil consumption A litre or more per 1,000 km, or blue smoke at startup or under load.
  • Coolant in the oil Beige or milky residue under the oil filler cap.
  • Structural rust Flaking, scaling metal at suspension pickup points on a salted car.
