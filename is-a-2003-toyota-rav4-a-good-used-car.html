Vehicle Intelligence · 2003 Toyota RAV4

Is a 2003 Toyota RAV4 a Good Used Car to Buy?

Yes. The second-generation RAV4 is one of the great high-mileage survivors — the only thing you really need to understand is a single automatic-transmission quirk from these model years, and it is cheap to catch early.

GOOD CAR

Why it's a good car

The 2003 RAV4 is the second-generation model (the XA20 chassis Toyota built from 2001 to 2005), and it runs the 2.0-litre 1AZ-FE four-cylinder from Toyota's AZ engine family. That is a genuinely global, mass-produced powertrain with a long paper trail and cheap parts available anywhere. It is mechanically simple, any independent shop can service it, and owners of this generation are unusually loyal for a vehicle this old. Reaching very high mileage is not a warning sign on this platform — it is the platform doing exactly what it is famous for.

Does it have a timing belt or a timing chain?

A timing chain. The 2.0-litre 1AZ-FE uses a chain rather than a belt, so there is no expensive scheduled timing service hanging over this engine — ever. That removes one of the biggest recurring costs you would otherwise face on a car of this age.

The one thing that defines these model years

On 2001–2003 RAV4s, a faulty engine control module can send bad signals to the automatic transmission, producing harsh shifting, flaring, and — if it is ignored long enough — genuine transmission damage. Toyota extended the warranty to cover it at the time, though that coverage has long since expired on any car this old. Here is the part that reframes it: caught early, the fix is to repair or replace the module, and specialist shops rebuild these units specifically for this car. It never has to touch the transmission. And a car that has already reached very high mileage has effectively passed the test — a slipping, ECM-killed transmission does not quietly get there. Feel the first few gear changes on a cold start; that is when the fault shows first and cleanest.

The things to check before you buy

  • Cold-start shift quality Drive it from stone cold and pay attention to the first three or four shifts. Smooth and quiet is what you want; any hard bang, flare, or hunting between gears when cold is the ECM talking, and it is the one thing worth catching early.
  • Oil level On any example this age, check the oil level and keep an eye on it between changes — an older engine that uses a little oil is fine as long as it is topped up and never run low.
  • Suspension and bushings Struts, sway-bar links, and control-arm bushings are 20-plus years old and are normal, priceable wear items on any example this age.
  • Cooling system Hoses, the radiator, and the water pump are all on borrowed time at high mileage if they have never been replaced.
  • Rust Coastal climates are kind to metal, but a truck that has lived somewhere salted will show it first at the frame rails, subframe mounts, and floor pans.

What would make you walk

  • Shifts that won't settle Hard banging, flaring, or gears that hunt and refuse to settle — that is the ECM fault progressing. Address it before it chews up the transmission.
  • Burnt transmission fluid Fluid that is dark brown or smells burnt suggests shifting damage may already be underway.
  • Smoke or fast oil loss Blue smoke on start-up, or oil disappearing far faster than a normal top-up, points to internal engine wear that changes the math on keeping it.
  • Structural rust Perforation through a frame rail, subframe mount, or floor pan is the one thing on this truck worth walking away from a repair over.
