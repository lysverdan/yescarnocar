Four Models, Twelve Reports, One Drive to Florida

How Nancy narrowed a field of used SUVs down to the one now towing her Airstream around Ontario.

Nancy summers in Ontario and winters in the Bahamas. Two places, two lives, one person who keeps them both running smoothly. The Airstream is an Ontario thing — something to pull on a summer weekend when the mood is right.

That puts real demands on a tow vehicle. Not "SUV-shaped" demands — actual towing capacity, a drivetrain that can be trusted at highway speed with several thousand pounds behind it, and a platform that isn't going to surprise her somewhere between campgrounds. Nancy wasn't looking for a car. She was looking for the right tool for a specific job, and she wasn't going to get that decision wrong.

She also wasn't in a hurry. Nancy is methodical about the things that matter — she does the work before she commits, not after. The Airstream tow vehicle was not going to be a gut-feel purchase. That one, she was going to earn.

Four Models on the List

Nancy didn't go to a dealer lot and start kicking tires. She went to Facebook Marketplace, and she went in with a shortlist already built — four models, each one a legitimate answer to the towing question, each with a different personality:

  • Porsche Cayenne — the outlier. A performance SUV with genuine towing numbers, if you found the right one and it had been cared for.
  • Mercedes ML350 — the workhorse. A midsize luxury SUV built with a truck-adjacent platform and a reputation for handling a load without complaint.
  • Audi Q7 — the daily-driver's choice. Comfortable, capable, and common enough that good used examples weren't hard to find.
  • Volkswagen Touareg — the Q7's cousin under the skin, often cheaper for the same bones.

Four models. All plausible. All available used, all around the country, all with wildly different histories depending on which specific car you were looking at. That's the part a shortlist can't tell you — it tells you what to shop for, not which individual listing is worth your time.

The System

Nancy worked Facebook Marketplace the way she seems to do most things that matter: methodically. She looked at hundreds of listings across the four models — not skimming, actually reading them, building a feel for what the market looked like and what asking prices actually meant for each model at each mileage range. From that, she pulled her top ten to fifteen. Those were the ones worth a real look. She ran a Yes Car No Car report on every single one before she ever picked up the phone about any of them.

Some of those listings came with a Carfax already attached. Wherever that was true, she dropped it in. And that's when the reports stopped being general and started being specific — the difference between a verdict built on the model and mileage in front of it, and a verdict that could speak to the actual history of the actual car. Ownership patterns. Where the miles had come from. What the record around that specific vehicle actually said, not just what tends to be true of an ML350 with those numbers on the odometer. Nancy noticed it happening in real time: hand it a Carfax, and the answer that came back knew more.

Hundreds of listings down to ten or fifteen. Ten or fifteen reports down to three cars worth driving to see. That's not luck. That's a system.

Cutting the List

The eliminations came fast once the reports were in front of her. The reports cleared two of the Touaregs almost immediately — nothing dramatic, just enough in the findings that they weren't worth pursuing next to the alternatives. One of the Cayennes came back with findings Nancy wasn't willing to negotiate around, and it came off the list the same day. A couple of the Q7s and ML350s were fine cars attached to prices that didn't make sense once she could see what she was actually buying.

She wasn't looking for a reason to say no to all of them. She was looking for the handful that could survive being looked at closely — and by the time she'd worked through the full list, that's exactly what she had left.

She didn't fall in love with a car and go looking for reasons to justify it. She let ten reports argue with each other until three were left standing.

Three Cars, One Trip to Florida

By the end of the process, Nancy had a top three — not a type of car, three specific vehicles, in specific places, with specific histories she already understood before she'd laid eyes on any of them. All three happened to be in Florida.

So she flew in from the Bahamas. Not to shop — to close. She already knew what each of the three cars was, and what each report had said about it. The trip wasn't reconnaissance. It was three appointments with cars she'd already vetted, to confirm in person what the data had already told her.

She bought the Mercedes ML350.

Verdict: Good Car — One Condition

The report on that ML350 had come back clean with a single condition attached — a specific, known point worth factoring into the deal, not a reason to walk away from it. Nancy knew about that condition before she opened the driver's door. She wasn't discovering it at the negotiating table. She was using it there.

Towing Now, Not Guessing

She bought the Mercedes, loaded up a rescue puppy she'd brought back from the Bahamas, and drove north to Ontario. The Airstream has a proper tow vehicle now. The puppy has a proper ride home. Nancy did not get lucky. She did the work — weeks earlier, in front of a laptop, across hundreds of listings and four different models — and the trip was just the last step of a process that had already told her what she'd find.

That's the story underneath the story: not a horror show, not a close call avoided at the last second. Just a big purchase, taken seriously, by someone who ran the numbers on every candidate instead of asking a friend or trusting a lot attendant. The verdicts did the filtering. Nancy did the driving.

Buying an SUV to actually use it? Run every candidate before you fall for one.
