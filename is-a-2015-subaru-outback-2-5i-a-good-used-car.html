Vehicle Intelligence · 2015 Subaru Outback 2.5i

Is a 2015 Subaru Outback 2.5i a Good Used Car to Buy?

Yes — with conditions. The 2015 is a sweet spot in the Outback line, but three things decide it: whether the engine burns oil, whether the CVT is healthy and served, and front wheel bearings.

GOOD CAR — WITH CONDITIONS

Why it's a good car

This is the fifth-generation Outback, and the 2.5i is the mainstream version: a 2.5-liter FB25 boxer four (about 175 hp), the Lineartronic CVT, and Subaru's symmetrical all-wheel drive. The FB25 runs a maintenance-free timing chain, so the classic Subaru timing-belt anxiety does not apply here. It is a genuinely durable, well-understood all-weather wagon that runs to high mileage when maintained, and Subaru extended the CVT warranty to 10 years / 100,000 miles on 2010–2015 Legacy and Outback models, which can still matter on some cars.

Does it have a timing belt or a timing chain?

A timing chain. The FB25 uses a maintenance-free chain, so there is no belt interval to budget for — a real advantage over older Subarus.

The things to check before you buy

  • Oil consumption Earlier FB-series engines — especially 2013–2014 — drew a class-action settlement over piston rings that wore early and burned oil. The 2015 Outback is a later, revised-production car and falls outside that settlement, but some owners still report consumption, so check it: pull the dipstick cold and ask the seller flat out how often it needs topping up. A quart every 1,000 km or so is a heavy burner and a reason to walk on this engine.
  • CVT Drive it from cold and again warm — it should pull away smoothly with no shudder, hesitation, or droning surge. Look for a fluid-service record (the "lifetime" fill is a myth) and check whether the extended CVT warranty still applies. A smooth, served CVT is a non-issue.
  • Front wheel bearings The single most-reported item on this platform. At higher mileage expect one or more to be near replacement — listen for a cyclic hum or growl that changes with steering load. Cheap, but price it into your offer.
  • Seal seepage The FB25 weeps a little oil from the cam-carrier and valve-cover seals with age — a light film is normal, an active drip onto the exhaust is worth pricing.
  • Rust On a salt or winter car, check the rear subframe, rear control-arm areas, and brake lines for corrosion.

What would make you walk

  • Heavy oil consumption A confirmed quart every ~1,000 km points to a short block on this engine family — a walk-away, not a negotiation.
  • A failing CVT Shudder, flare, or stored transmission codes.
  • Structural rust Corrosion through the subframe, control-arm mounts, or brake lines.
