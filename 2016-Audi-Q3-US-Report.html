The full picture
The Depreciation Story

This is where luxury depreciation becomes your opportunity. A 2016 Q3 2.0T Premium Plus quattro stickered around $35,000 new and now sits near $11,000–$13,500 — it lost most of its value in its first several years, and you're buying at the bottom. The badge that made it expensive new makes it a bargain used, provided you go in understanding what German ownership at this age asks of you.

What the Car Actually Is

This is the first-generation Q3 (the 8U, sold in North America 2015–2018), Audi's entry luxury crossover, built on the same MQB-era mechanicals as the VW Tiguan and Golf of its day. Crucially for the U.S. car: it pairs the EA888 2.0 TFSI with a conventional 6-speed Tiptronic automatic and quattro — a simpler, more robust drivetrain than the dual-clutch setups found elsewhere in the lineup.

Engine and Drivetrain

The 2.0T EA888 is a strong, well-understood turbocharged four with a deep support network. Its known maintenance items are the point of this report: the timing-chain tensioner (early versions could fail and let the chain skip — confirm the updated part is installed), the plastic-impeller water pump, intake-valve carbon from direct injection, and the PCV system. Paired with the 6-speed Tiptronic — a real torque-converter automatic — and quattro, it's a durable combination when those items are kept up. Real-world economy runs about 22–24 mpg on premium fuel. This is not a tow vehicle.

What Breaks and What It Means

Nothing here is a systemic, uninspectable defect — every concern is a known EA888 maintenance item you can verify before buying. The one that carries real stakes is the timing-chain tensioner: fitted with the updated part, the risk is retired; left on an old tensioner, a failure can let the chain jump and cause four-figure engine damage. The water pump and carbon cleaning are routine at this mileage and cost. The whole game is confirming the tensioner and getting a clean scan.

Red Flags

The tensioner is the flag that matters — confirm the revised part by number or service invoice, not by ear alone. Pair that with a clean VCDS scan (no engine, emissions, or quattro faults) and evidence the water pump and thermostat were done. Then confirm the brake-light/parking-brake recall (Audi 97DK) is closed by VIN. An owner without records on a German car this age is a reason to price in the worst, not assume the best.

Driveway Inspection

Cold start and listen for a momentary rattle before oil pressure builds — the tensioner tell. Check the oil level and the filler-cap underside. On the drive, the Tiptronic should shift cleanly and quattro should feel planted with no binding on tight turns. Above all, arrange a VCDS scan through an independent VW/Audi shop before committing — on this platform the scan is worth more than anything you'll feel from the driver's seat.

Upcoming Maintenance Outlook

At ~90,000 miles, plan for the next year or two: a carbon walnut blast if not recently done (~$500–$800), water pump and thermostat if there's no record (~$600–$900), spark plugs and coils as due (~$300–$500), a DSG-free transmission service on the Tiptronic (~$250–$400), Haldex/quattro service (~$150–$250), and brake fluid (~$100–$150). Premium fuel is a running cost. These are the price of a German luxury vehicle at this mileage — budget for them like a partner, not an afterthought.

Three Year Cost Projection

Budget roughly $4,500 over three years at about 12,000 miles a year, assuming the tensioner is confirmed updated and the scan is clean at purchase. That covers routine oil services, the maintenance items above, one brake service, tires, and a realistic buffer for the small electrical and trim items that come with any German car this age. It excludes fuel and insurance, and it assumes no major engine or turbo work — which is exactly what the pre-purchase checks exist to rule out.

Who This Car Is Actually For

The buyer who wants a genuinely premium small SUV at a bargain price and has either the budget headroom to absorb a surprise four-figure repair or a good relationship with an independent VW/Audi shop. It's a sharp buy for the right owner — and an expensive mistake for anyone treating it as a frugal, worry-free commuter.

The Verdict

GOOD CAR — WITH CONDITIONS. The conditions are specific and all inspectable before you hand over a dollar: the timing-chain tensioner is the updated part (confirmed by part number, not by ear), the water pump and thermostat have recent service, and a VCDS scan is clean with oil consumption in check. Make each a written condition of the sale. If they come back clean, this is a fully-loaded luxury crossover for the price of a used compact. If one fails, negotiate the cost off using the figures above, or walk. There's nothing special enough about any single Q3 to buy it without these protections — they're the difference between a smart buy and the money pit the badge is famous for.
