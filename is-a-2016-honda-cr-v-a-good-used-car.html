Vehicle Intelligence · 2016 Honda CR-V

Is a 2016 Honda CR-V a Good Used Car to Buy?

Yes. The last year of the fourth generation has the engine you want, none of the next generation's turbo troubles, and nothing systemic hanging over it — just fluids to keep honest and, on salt cars, an underbody to inspect.

GOOD CAR

Why it's a good car

The 2016 CR-V is the fourth generation in its refreshed 2015–2016 form: the direct-injected 2.4-liter Earth Dreams four (the K24W, about 185 hp) with a CVT. Two accidents of timing work strongly in its favor. It is naturally aspirated, so the oil-dilution saga that dogged the 1.5-liter turbo in the 2017-and-later fifth generation simply doesn't apply. And it is a different generation from the 2007–2011 trucks covered by the notorious rear-frame rust recall. What is left is the recipe the CR-V's reputation was built on: a durable engine that routinely clears 300,000 km with clean oil, cheap parts, and competent labor everywhere.

Does it have a timing belt or a timing chain?

A timing chain. The K24W uses a chain — no belt interval, no scheduled timing service, ever.

One launch-year footnote

The engine-and-CVT combination was new for 2015, and that first year drew significant driveline-vibration complaints that Honda chased with software and hardware updates. The 2016 was much improved, though a few owners still reported vibration at idle — worth noting on a test drive, not worth fearing. The formal fixes applied to the 2015 model year specifically.

The things to check before you buy

  • CVT fluid history The single most important maintenance item on this car. Honda's CVT is robust — far more so than the units that gave the technology its bad name — but it lives on fluid. Confirm it has been serviced with genuine Honda HCF-2 fluid, ideally more than once by higher mileage.
  • Underbody rust On a salt-province car, put a flashlight on the rear subframe, trailing-arm mounts, brake and fuel lines, and floor pans. Surface scale is normal at this age; flaking or perforation is the one conversation-ender.
  • Cold-start rattle A brief metallic rattle in the first second or two is the K24-family VTC actuator — a known trait, mostly noise. A loud, persistent clatter deserves a closer look.
  • Air conditioning No documented pattern on this generation (the formal Honda A/C campaigns cover the 2017-and-later trucks), but test it properly anyway — five minutes on max cold.
  • Oil level and drive feel High-kilometre K24s can use a little oil, so check the dipstick habit; and on the drive, confirm the CVT pulls smoothly with no shudder, hunting, or flare.

What would make you walk

  • Structural rust Flaking or perforated metal at the rear subframe, trailing-arm mounts, or floor pans.
  • A worn CVT Shudder, slip, or hunting under steady throttle that a proper fluid service doesn't cure.
  • A tired bottom end Deep metallic knock under load, or oil consumption approaching a liter every 1,000 km.
